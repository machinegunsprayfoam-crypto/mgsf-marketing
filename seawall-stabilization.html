How a seawall really fails

The wall usually isn't the first thing to go — the soil behind it is. Wave action, water level swings, and drainage push water through joints and gaps, and it carries the backfill out with it. That leaves voids behind the wall, the ground above sinks, and with nothing holding it, the wall itself starts to lean and crack. Polyurethane injection gets ahead of that: we fill the voids, seal the water paths moving the soil, and re-support the wall — without ripping it out and rebuilding.

🕳️ Fills the voids

💧 Seals water paths

Common questions

Will this fix a wall that's already leaning?

It depends how far it's gone. Injection re-supports the soil and seals the water paths, which stops the cause and can stabilize a wall that's started to move — but a wall that's structurally failed may be past injection. We'll assess it and tell you straight, not sell you a fix that won't hold.

Does the foam hold up around water?

Closed-cell polyurethane is hydrophobic — it's designed to cure and seal in wet conditions, which is why it works behind walls where water is the whole problem.

Do you have to tear the wall out?

No — that's the advantage. Injection goes in through small ports behind or through the wall, so you keep the wall and skip the tear-out and rebuild.
