1. Assembly design — spec foam where it earns its keep

All-foam everywhere is usually over-spec'd. We optimize the assembly so closed-cell does the air/vapor/structural work and cheaper fill carries the rest of the R.

Walls — flash-and-fill (hybrid)

  • 1.5–2″ ccSPF direct to sheathing: continuous air + vapor control, plus it bonds sheathing to framing (racking / load-path stiffness).
  • Fill the remaining cavity with fiberglass or dense-pack cellulose to reach the assembly R-target.
  • Result: foam-grade air sealing + structure, batt-grade cost on the balance of the R.

Roofs — unvented deck (IRC R806.5)

  • ccSPF to the underside of the roof deck: conditioned attic, deck protection, and an adhesive bond that adds wind-uplift resistance beyond fasteners alone.
  • In cold climates the ccSPF layer must be thick enough to keep the condensing surface warm if air-permeable insulation is added below it — under-spraying that ratio is the #1 cause of hybrid-roof moisture problems.
Verify: wind-uplift ratings require an engineer + a tested assembly. Condensation-control ccSPF ratios and R-targets are code- and climate-zone-specific — confirm the adopted IECC/IRC edition and any local amendments with the AHJ before it goes in writing.

3. Code barriers — budget them, don't get caught

  • Thermal barrier over foam in occupied space: 15-minute rated — ½″ gypsum or an approved coating (e.g. DC315). (IRC R316.4 / IBC 2603.4)
  • Ignition barrier in attics/crawlspaces without regular access, or an approved coating. (IRC R316.5)
  • ICC-ES report (ESR/ER): we provide the evaluation report for the specific foam so the inspector has the code basis in hand.
Section numbers renumber between code editions. Confirm the edition your AHJ has adopted, plus local amendments.

4. Applicator PPE & exposure control (SPFA-aligned)

SPF chemistry (MDI isocyanate + amine catalyst + blowing agent) is hazardous during spray and cure. Our protocol:

  • Respiratory: supplied-air respirator (SAR) for interior high-pressure spraying; cartridge APR (organic-vapor + P100) only where an exposure assessment and ventilation support it.
  • Skin/eye: full coverage suit + hood, nitrile gloves, sealed goggles — MDI is a skin/respiratory sensitizer.
  • Lift discipline: spray to the manufacturer's max lift thickness in multiple passes. Over-thick lifts trap exothermic heat → scorching, degraded cell structure, and extra off-gassing — worse for both quality and exposure.
  • Ventilation + re-occupancy: ventilate during and after; honor the product's re-entry time (per TDS) before other trades return.

5. Equipment-verified quality control

Realized R-value = the product's rated R × install quality. We control the variables that decide in-situ cell structure and adhesion:

  • Ratio: 1:1 A:B, with daily ratio / AB-draw checks. Off-ratio foam = poor cell structure, friability, odor, lost R and adhesion.
  • Temperature: drum, heated-hose, and substrate temps to spec; substrate at least 5°F above dew point (no-go under 5°F spread).
  • Pressure: balanced A/B at the proportioner.
  • Thickness verification: depth checks / cores to confirm installed thickness meets the R-target.
  • Air-sealing proof: blower-door ACH50, before/after where the scope calls for it.
On "nominal vs. verified" R: code sets R by the product's rated (aged/LTTR) value — that stays. The real-world gap is install quality, and it's closed cheaply by verifying installed thickness + air sealing (blower door), not by lab-testing every job. That documentation is also the proof an owner or inspector can actually trust.
